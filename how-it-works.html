// Page 02 — How AI Phishing Works

Inside the Attack

AI has given cybercriminals an unprecedented arsenal — from flawless emails to cloned voices and deepfake videos. Learn exactly how these attacks are engineered.

// 01 — The Toolkit

How Attackers Use AI

AI gives attackers the tools they need that were previously out of reach. Tools like ChatGPT can create messages that appear to be from real people or companies and are professionally written and grammatically correct — something that was previously thought impossible. With generative AI, attackers only need a few pieces of information about their target to orchestrate a convincing attack.

// 02 — Attack Methods

Three Ways AI Powers Phishing

01 //

Spear Phishing at Scale

AI analyzes publicly available data about a target — social media, company websites, news — and crafts highly personalized messages. What once took hours per victim now takes seconds per thousands.

02 //

Voice Cloning

With as little as 3 seconds of audio, AI can clone an individual's voice. Attackers use recordings from calls, podcasts, or public videos to create fake urgent voice messages convincing victims to act.

03 //

Deepfake Video

Deepfake technology allows attackers to create realistic video content mimicking trusted individuals — used to trick victims into transferring funds or revealing sensitive information.

// 03 — Beyond the Inbox

Deepfakes and Voice Cloning

AI has also brought about new forms of phishing that go beyond the email. With voice cloning technology, it is possible to clone an individual's voice with as little as 3 seconds of audio. Additionally, deepfake technology allows for the creation of videos of an individual that can be used to trick individuals into transferring their funds or revealing sensitive information. These attacks can arrive through phone calls, video calls, and messaging apps — not just email.

// 04 — Recognition

Warning Signs to Watch Out For

Since AI phishing messages do not feature the same mistakes as human phishing messages, recognizing them requires paying attention to the behavior of the message. Phishing messages typically contain each of the following characteristics:

  • Threatening language that creates a sense of urgency regarding the required reader response
  • A request for personal and sensitive information from the reader
  • An email address that is slightly off from the real email address of the organization
  • Links that do not match the organization's actual website
  • Attachments to the message from an unknown sender
  • A personal tone to the message that uses the reader's name or references their activities for which they are responsible
